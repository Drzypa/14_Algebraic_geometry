irreducible


A subset F of a topological space X is reducible if it can be written as a union F=F1∪F2 of two closed proper subsets F1, F2 of F (closed in the subspace topology). That is, F is reducible if it can be written as a union F=(G1∩F)∪(G2∩F) where G1,G2 are closed subsets of X, neither of which contains F.

A subset of a topological space is irreducible (or hyperconnected) if it is not reducible.

As an example, consider {(x,y)∈ℝ2:x⁢y=0} with the subspace topology from ℝ2. This space is a union of two lines {(x,y)∈ℝ2:x=0} and {(x,y)∈ℝ2:y=0}, which are proper closed subsets. So this space is reducible, and thus not irreducible.
