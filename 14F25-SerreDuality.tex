The most general version of Serre duality states that on certain schemes $X$ of dimension $n$, including all projective varieties over any algebraically closed field $k$, there is a natural perfect \PMlinkname{pairing}{BilinearMap}
\[
  \Ext^{i}(\mathcal{F},\omega)\times H^{n-i}(X,\mathcal{F})\to k,
\]
where $\mathcal{F}$ is any coherent sheaf on $X$ and $\omega$ is a \PMlinkescapetext{fixed} sheaf, called the \emph{dualizing sheaf}.  Here ``perfect'' means that the natural map above induces an isomorphism
\[
  \Ext^{i}(\mathcal{F},\omega)\cong \Hom{H^{n-i}(X,\mathcal{F})}{k}.
\]

In special cases, this reduces to more approachable forms.  If $X$ is nonsingular (or more generally, Cohen-Macaulay), then $\omega$ is simply $\bigwedge^n\Omega$, where $\Omega$ is the sheaf of differentials on $X$.

If $\mathcal{F}$ is locally free, then $$\Ext^i(\mathcal{F},\omega)\cong\Ext^i(\O_X,\mathcal{F}^*\otimes\omega)\cong
H^i(X,\mathcal{F}^*\otimes\omega),$$ so that we obtain the somewhat more familiar looking fact that there is a perfect pairing $H^i(X,\mathcal{F}^*\otimes\omega)\times H^{n-i}(X,\mathcal{F})\to k$.

While Serre duality is not in a strict sense a generalization of Poincar\'e duality, they are philosophically similar, and both fit into a larger pattern on duality results.
