regular ideal


An ideal 𝔞 of a ring R is called a , iff 𝔞 a regular element of R.

Proposition.  If m is a positive integer, then the only regular ideal in the residue class ring ℤm is the unit ideal (1).

Proof.  The ring ℤm is a principal ideal ring.  Let (n) be any regular ideal of the ring ℤm.  Then n can not be zero divisor, since otherwise there would be a non-zero element r of ℤm such that  n⁢r=0  and thus every element s⁢n of the principal ideal would satisfy  (s⁢n)⁢r=s⁢(n⁢r)=s⁢0=0.  So, n is a regular element of ℤm and therefore we have  gcd⁡(m,n)=1.  Then, according to Bézout’s lemma (http://planetmath.org/BezoutsLemma), there are such integers x and y that  1=x⁢m+y⁢n.  This equation gives the congruence  1≡y⁢n(modm),  i.e.  1=y⁢n  in the ring ℤm.  With  1 the principal ideal (n) contains all elements of ℤm, which means that  (n)=ℤm=(1).

Note.  The above notion of “regular ideal” is used in most books concerning ideals of commutative rings, e.g. [1].  There is also a different notion of “regular ideal” mentioned in [2] (p. 179):  Let I be an ideal of the commutative ring R with non-zero unity.  This ideal is called regular, if the quotient ring R/I is a regular ring, in other words, if for each  a∈R  there exists an element  b∈R  such that  a2⁢b-a∈I.
